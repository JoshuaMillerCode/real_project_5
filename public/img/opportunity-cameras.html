Farewell Opportunity rover!

“Thanks Oppy, for being the little rover that could!” This tweeted message from NASA marks the end of operation for a rather special space rover, Opportunity.

When it began life on Mars, in 2004, a 90-day mission was planned to explore 1,000 meters. Remarkably, this turned into 15 years operation and 45 kilometers of travel

It helped provide ground-breaking discoveries, says NASA. For example, it discovered definitive proof of liquid water on ancient Mars and it also set the off-world driving record.

#ThanksOppy for being the little rover that could! A planned 90-day mission to explore Mars turned into 15 years of ground-breaking discoveries and record-breaking achievements. Here's a look: https://t.co/e32XC64e3C

— NASA (@NASA) February 14, 2019

After 800 attempts to re-establish contact, communication attempts have been abandoned. It is believed a severe Mars-wide dust storm blanketed its location back in June 2018. Time has now been called.

“It is because of trailblazing missions such as Opportunity that there will come a day when our brave astronauts walk on the surface of Mars,” said NASA Administrator Jim Bridenstine. “And when that day arrives, some portion of that first footprint will be owned by the men and women of Opportunity, and a little rover that defied the odds and did so much in the name of exploration.”

Opportunity achievements

  • Set a one-day Mars driving record March 20, 2005, when it travelled 721 feet (220 meters).
  • Returned more than 217,000 images, including 15 360-degree colour panoramas.
  • Exposed the surfaces of 52 rocks to reveal fresh mineral surfaces for analysis and cleared 72 additional targets with a brush to prepare them for inspection with spectrometers and a microscopic imager.
  • Found hematite, a mineral that forms in water, at its landing site.
  • Discovered strong indications at Endeavour Crater of the action of ancient water similar to the drinkable water of a pond or lake on Earth.

It’s final, dust-covered resting spot? Perseverance Valley. It landed originally in the Meridiani Planum region of Mars on January 24, 2004, seven months after its launch from Cape Canaveral Air Force Station in Florida.

“I cannot think of a more appropriate place for Opportunity to endure on the surface of Mars than one called Perseverance Valley,” said Michael Watkins, director of JPL. “The records, discoveries and sheer tenacity of this intrepid little rover is testament to the ingenuity, dedication, and perseverance of the people who built and guided her.”

So much credit, then, to NASA’s Jet Propulsion Laboratory, which managed the Mars Exploration Rovers Opportunity.
